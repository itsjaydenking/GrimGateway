Building Your Legend

Welcome to character creation in Light Omens. Here, every choice shapes your character’s journey and the legacy they leave behind. In the world of Light Omens, heroes, adventurers, and scholars often leave their mark on those who follow. Whether through Reminiscent Bonds with companions or Memorial Bonds with the fallen, your character’s story can inspire future generations. As you build your character, a seeker should consider the impact they hope to have and how their memory might guide others, even when they are no longer there to lead. You are not just creating a character for adventures but shaping a piece of the world’s history.

Attributes

A character’s attributes stand for their core abilities. They express how capable a character is when trying specific actions. Each attribute has a rating from 1 to 6, measuring a character's physical, mental, and social aspects.

Body

The Body attribute encapsulates a character’s physical prowess, encompassing strength, agility, and endurance. It gauges their physical strength, dexterity, and stamina. Examples of actions include lifting weights, balancing on a beam, or swimming long distances.

Body Attribute Examples

  1. Weak Body (1). Struggles with basic physical tasks, has trouble lifting moderately heavy objects like a full bag of supplies, and is often winded after running short distances.
  2. Decent Body (2). Handles most physical activities with some effort; can lift and carry a pack, climb short distances, and run a moderate distance before tiring.
  3. Strong Body (3). Confident with physical challenges; capable of heavy lifting, sprinting for longer periods, and showing impressive resilience in rough terrain.
  4. Exceptional Body (4). Outshines the average person physically; able to lift and carry another person easily, sprint long distances without fatigue, and handle intense physical exertion.
  5. Outstanding Body (5). Capable of near-superhuman feats, can break down a sturdy door, swim through rough currents, or sustain powerful, precise movements in combat.
  6. Legendary Body (6). Possesses extraordinary strength and agility; can bend metal bars, perform complex acrobatics effortlessly, or withstand prolonged exposure to extreme environments without issue.

Mind

The Mind attribute embodies a character’s mental faculties, encompassing intelligence, wisdom, and concentration. It measures their cognitive abilities, problem-solving skills, and mental strength. Examples of actions include solving puzzles, perceiving traps, or quick thinking.

Mind Attribute Examples

  1. Weak Mind (1). Struggles with complex ideas; may miss subtleties in puzzles or get easily confused with detailed plans or instructions.
  2. Decent Mind (2). Understands basic concepts and can hold their own in simple problem-solving; notices obvious clues and makes straightforward connections.
  3. Strong Mind (3). Able to process information quickly and efficiently; good at analyzing situations, solving moderately complex puzzles, and recognizing patterns in data.
  4. Exceptional Mind (4). Exhibits high mental acuity and focus; can easily notice hidden details, solve complex problems quickly, and stay mentally sharp in stressful situations.
  5. Outstanding Mind (5). Possesses incredible insight and reasoning; capable of unraveling intricate puzzles, detecting subtle deceptions, and planning complex strategies.
  6. Legendary Mind (6). Has a mind capable of near-superhuman processing power; can devise multi-layered plans on the fly, spot faint clues easily, and reason through the most challenging dilemmas.

Soul

The Soul attribute reveals a character’s ability to be honest, convincing, and resolute. It shows their ability to navigate social interactions and challenges. Examples of actions include intimidating creatures, deceiving guards, or staying calm in danger.

Soul Attribute Examples

  1. Weak Soul (1): Struggles with self-assurance and influencing others, have difficulty asserting themselves, may get flustered in confrontations, or are easily intimidated.
  2. Decent Soul (2): Has a basic level of social awareness, can hold their ground in conversations and understand familiar social cues, though they may not excel at persuasion.
  3. Strong Soul (3): Confident in social interactions; can persuade or intimidate with some effectiveness, and remains calm in most high-stakes social situations.
  4. Exceptional Soul (4): Displays charisma and conviction; can sway crowds, remain composed under pressure, and talk their way out of tough situations with finesse.
  5. Outstanding Soul (5): Commands attention effortlessly; can manipulate or inspire others on a large scale, resist intense psychological pressures, and remain focused through mental strain.
  6. Legendary Soul (6): Has a nearly supernatural presence and emotional resilience; capable of rallying people to seemingly impossible causes, unflinching in the face of fear, and able to empathize with or even manipulate the emotions of others to a profound degree.

Approaches

A character’s approaches stand for a character’s training and understanding. They express how effective a character is in a specific action. Each approach has a rating from 0 to 4, measuring a character's preference for being direct, indirect, or enduring.

Below are examples of what a character might accomplish at each level of the Force, Finesse, and Focus Approaches. These examples help you gauge how your character’s skill level impacts their success in different scenarios.

Force

Force represents direct, straightforward action. Characters using force rely on physical power, straightforward problem-solving, and sincerity.

Using Force

Use force when time is of the essence or when the situation requires immediate, impactful action that bypasses complexity. Force works best when subtly isn’t necessary or when brute strength or decisiveness will achieve results more efficiently.

Finesse

Finesse involves precision, skill, and subtly. Characters using finesse emphasize agility, accuracy, and careful planning.

Using Finesse

Use finesse for situations where control and accuracy are crucial. It is the go-to approach when a plan needs a subtle or delicate touch or when you want to achieve a goal without creating unnecessary chaos.

Focus

Focus represents resilience, patience, and endurance. Characters with focus rely on their ability to maintain control and persist through difficulty.

Using Focus

Focus is helpful in situations requiring sustained effort or where a calm, controlled approach can prevent errors or fatigue. It’s beneficial when staying power and endurance are more valuable than speed or power.

Methods

Methods represent a character’s general behavior and express how particular a character is in a specific action. Each method combines an attribute and an approach, with rankings from 0 to 6. Each method has a descriptor that describes a particular action more specifically. When using strength, Body (Force), a character performs a brutal action.

Using Methods. Ranks in a method allow you to upgrade your base dice pool. When rolling for a specific method, replace up to [X] d6 in your dice pool with d12s, where X is the smallest of your attribute and approach rating. You can never upgrade more dice than your attribute and cannot upgrade any dice if you are hopeless.

Body (Approach) Methods

Body (Approach) methods involve performing physical actions in a specific way. The following methods relate to these physical actions.

  • Strength. Body (Force) describes Brutal methods. Behaving brutally applies when using strength and force.
  • Dexterity. Body (Finesse) describes Nimble methods. Behaving nimbly applies when using dexterity and leverage.
  • Stamina. Body (Focus) describes Study methods. Behaving sturdily applies when using stamina or resilience.

Mind (Approach) Methods

Mind (Approach) methods involve performing mental actions in a specific way. The following methods relate to these mental actions.

  • Intelligence. Mind (Force) describes Rational methods. Behaving rationally applies when using intelligence and logic.
  • Wisdom. Mind (Finesse) describes Insightful methods. Behaving insightfully applies when using wisdom and awareness.
  • Concentration. Mind (Focus) describes Deliberate methods. Behaving deliberately applies when using concentration and memory.

Soul (Approach) Methods

Soul (Approach) methods involve performing social actions in a specific way. The following methods relate to these social actions.

  • Sincerity. Soul (Force) describes Earnest methods. Behaving earnestly applies when using sincerity and honesty.
  • Influence. Soul (Finesse) describes Cunning methods. Behaving cunningly applies when using influence and manipulation.
  • Resolve. Soul (Focus) describes Graceful methods. Behaving gracefully applies when using resolve and self-control.

Hope

Player characters have access to a personal supply called Hope. Hope stands for the elements that make them stand out. Hope allows a character to push their limits and achieve grand feats. Characters may alter the world around them and avoid negative consequences. Characters may have abilities that need Hope to perform them. Hope is the extent of a character's will to the world and will to live.

Spending Hope

Each player character has their own Hope pool set up during character creation. There are a few ways a character may spend their Hope.

  • Upgrade Dice. A character may spend Hope to upgrade a single d6. This choice can only apply to the value of the character’s Approach. This costs one Hope per upgrade.
  • Reroll Dice. A character may spend Hope to reroll up to two dice in their dice pool. This costs one Hope.
  • Avoid Injury. A character may spend Hope to negate a negative consequence or impending Injury. Each Injury varies on the Hope cost.
  • Talents. Characters may learn Talents that offer extra ways to spend Hope.

Hopelessness

Hopelessness is a truth that occurs when a character’s hope reaches 0. Hopelessness is temporary and occurs if characters push themselves too far. A character can choose one of the different forms of hopelessness when their Hope becomes 0. Hopeless characters lock down one approach depending on the type of hopelessness. A character cannot use any d6s from their approach and cannot upgrade any dice with that approach. Hopeless characters also generate one Complication when using any alternative approach. A hopeless character cannot regain Hope by spending Harmony or generating Chaos. Regaining Hope doesn’t remove a character’s hopeless state.

  • Weakened characters lock down their Force approach. The character can no longer rely on direct, decisive actions. Physically intense tasks become exhausting or impossible, and their intimidation tactics lose impact. This forces the character to adapt using more subtle or enduring methods.
  • Clumsy characters lock down their Finesse approach. They struggle with precision, losing their ability to perform delicate tasks or agile maneuvers. They may have to resort to brute strength or simply tough it out.
  • Distracted characters lock down their Focus approach. They find it difficult to stay composed, losing their endurance and resilience. They may struggle to hold their ground and can be more easily shaken.

Handling Hopelessness

A character loses the hopeless state after completing any rest. An ally can remove a character’s hopeless state using a Plan and Prepare action to affect a Truth. Affecting the hopeless state is a Difficulty 2 Test. Removing the hopeless state from any ally recovers one Hope for each character involved. A character who gains the hopeless state gains experience. A character who helps another recover from the hopeless state also gains experience.

Technique Level

Every character has a Technique Level (TL). TL describes a character’s affinity to develop or interact with objects or tactics with varying complexities. This applies to physical objects, magical elements, and technical aspects of the world. Characters can interact with objects with a Complexity lower than or equal to their TL. Tests may still be necessary in difficult or dangerous situations.

Complicated Techniques

Characters without the proper TL face increased challenges when using an object. The Difficulty of a Test interacting with the object increases by +2 for each difference. A character may try to interact with the object with help. Only a character with the proper Technique Level may function as an assistant. The difficulty of the Test is only increased by +1 for each level difference. Characters trying to use an object without the Technique Level also generate Chaos. The Chaos generated equals the difference between the Complexity and the Technique Level.

Collaborative Techniques

A character may train another character on how to use an object during a Long Rest. The trainer must have a technique level that is high enough to use the object. The receiving character must have a Technique Level lower than the object's. The receiving character receives the mentored truth. They treat the Technique Level for the object as one lower level. A character may only have the mentored truth for a single object.

Supply Points

Every character collects salvaged components, harvested materials, or residual arcane energy. These supplies may assemble into tools, items, or situational effects. Player characters have a pool of Supply Points to stand for this.

Using Supply Points

A character may declare they have supplies that help when acting. They may declare this regardless of whether the action requires a Test. A character describes the supply they are revealing, crafting, or conjuring. The Game Master (GM) decides how many Supply Points the creation requires. The character gains the object as equipment Truth if they have enough supplies to pay the price. The character may try to resolve the action with the new object. A character cannot spend more Supply Points than their Technique Level on a single creation. Anything created this way has a Complexity level equal to one less the cost. Also, anything made using Supply Points breaks down at the end of the scene.

Supply Point Costs
Supply Points Description
1 A basic supply requiring no more than two components or ingredients.
2 A common supply requiring a few components or ingredients.
3 An uncommon supply requiring several components or ingredients.
4 A complex supply requiring several specialized components or ingredients.
5 A rare supply requiring several sophisticated components or ingredients.

Gathering Supply Points

Once per scene, a player may request to gather supplies. They decide whether to collect from natural, magical, or industrial sources. A character must make an Attribute (Force / Finesse) Test with a Difficulty of 1. On success, the character gains supplies equal to the number of dice rolled for the check (+1 for each d12 rolled). A character may spend one Harmony to increase the amount gained by +1. Places scarce in a specific supply may reduce the amount achieved in half (rounded up). Locations with plenty of particular supplies may double the amount earned. Any Supply Points a character gains above their baseline creates the encumbered Truth. A character can only carry a Supply Point equal to twice their baseline.

Bonds

Every player character has some connection to other group members. Two kinds of bonds form a Truth: friendships and rivalries. A character receives one or more bonds during character creation. These bonds aid in connecting the player characters within a group. These bonds help characters recover Hope while providing Truth.

Friendships

If a character has a friendly bond with another character, they tend to be more likely to help the other. When a character spends Harmony to Gain Favor or provides Hope during a rest for a friend, that ally regains +1 extra Hope.

Rivalries

If a character has a rivalry bond with another character, they tend to push one another to improve. When a character regains Hope by generating Chaos to Tempt Fate or spending Harmony to Gain Favor, their rivals regain +1 Hope.

Talents

A character’s attributes, approaches, and methods do not explain all that they are. Talents are extra benefits and abilities of a character. Each one grants some mechanical benefits that apply within a set of circumstances.

Putting it Together

When a player creates a character for Light Omens RPG, follow the following steps.

  1. Select a single Origin for the character. The origin describes where the character came from. This is a cultural aspect more than anything.
  2. Select an Archetype for the character. The archetype describes what the character does. This is about their career and primary skill set.
  3. Select a Tendency for the character. The tendency describes the character’s default behavior.

These three elements describe a character; you are a(n) tendency + origin + archetype.

Origins

Origins describe the beginnings of a character from birth and their cultural environment. The starting origins of Light Omens RPG are Aethernaut, Erathan, Mythoteric, Revenite, and Technocrat. Each origin provides a base set of benefits:

  • Truth. The character gains a single Truth, usually the name of the origin.
  • Attributes. The character’s attributes start at 1. The origin provides a starting attribute bonus.
  • Technique Level. The character’s Technique Level starts at 0. The origin may provide a starting technique level bonus.
  • Hope. The character’s maximum Hope begins at 1. The origin may provide a starting Hope bonus.
  • Supplies. The character’s baseline Supplies begins at 2. The origin may provide a baseline Supply bonus.
  • Special Abilities. The origin provides the character with one or more special abilities. These abilities derive from knowledge and training of that origin.

Unknown Origins

Sometimes a character has mysterious origins. They could have amnesia, lost access to their original form, or otherwise not be part of this world. This means the character starts without using one of the established origins available. This is an advanced choice; a player should get the Game Master's (GM's) permission first. The character starts with 16 experiences to spend. These points may only increase Attributes, Technique Level, Hope, and Supplies. Additionally, the character may choose one Special Ability from any available origin.

Archetypes

Archetypes describe the training that a character has experienced in recent years. These are distinct from a character’s culture and have several influences. The starting archetypes of Light Omens RPG are arbiter, broker, harvester, runner, vanguard, and wright. Each archetype provides a base set of benefits:

  • Truth. The character gains a single Truth, usually the name of the archetype.
  • Approaches. The character’s attributes start at 0. The archetype provides starting approach bonuses.
  • Technique Level. The archetype may provide another starting Technique Level bonus.
  • Hope. The archetype may provide another starting maximum Hope bonus.
  • Supplies. The archetype may provide another starting baseline Supply bonus.
  • Goals. The archetype has a specific goal that gives them the experience to grow from.
  • Talents. The archetype has a list of talents from which only members of that archetype may choose. A character chooses one of the listed Talents as their starting Talent.
  • Equipment. The archetype provides a few pieces of equipment. Some archetypes offer a choice between several items. A player cannot pick items with a Complexity higher than their Technique Level.

Unusual Archetypes

Some characters get unique training over their life. They could have an exceptional mentor, borrow knowledge, or otherwise not be part of this world. This means the character starts without using one of the established origins available. This is an advanced choice; a player should get the Game Master's (GM's) permission first. The character starts with 14 experiences to spend. These points may only increase Approaches, Technique Levels, Hope, and Supplies.

Additionally, they begin with two Talents of their choice from the General Talents. Such a character does not start with goals, wealth, or equipment, but the GM may choose to provide wealth or equipment.

Tendencies

Tendencies are not personalities but more character behaviors. The available tendencies are aggressive, analytical, devious, empathetic, expressive, or stubborn. Each tendency provides a base set of benefits:

  • Truth. The character gains a single Truth, usually the name of the tendency.
  • Method. The character’s methods start with 0 ranks. The tendency provides the character with a single Method rank related to a specific approach.
  • Bond. The character starts with several bonds. One of the starting bonds must be a specific type.
  • Drive. The tendency provides the character with a specific behavior that gives a personal way to recover Hope.
  • Hopelessness. The tendency has a particular type of Hopelessness that a character experiences from reduced to zero Hope.
  • Flaw. The tendency provides the character with a specific behavior that gives a personal way to gain experience.

Expanding Characters

Once you have your origin, archetype, and tendency, it is time to finish the final details. Also, explore the ways to improve your character moving forward.

Calculating Hope

You should have your maximum Hope value. This equals the number of your method [Attribute (Approach)] chosen from your tendency. Any bonuses from your origin and archetype further increase it. Remember, characters all have a starting +1 for their Hope.

Calculating Supply Points

You should have your baseline Supplies value. This equals the bonuses gained from your origin and archetype. Remember, characters all have a starting +2 for their baseline Supplies.

Establishing Bonds

The number of bonds you have depends on the number of players in the group. Your tendency is either based on friendship or rivalry. Choose one other player character in the group to share a bond with that type. This bond goes both ways and is the same for both characters. Any subsequent bonds gained by any characters can be a friendship or rivalry.

Starting Equipment

Your chosen archetype gives you your starting equipment. Supply Points represent other objects, items, and improvised equipment. Each starting character begins with 2 Wealth Points. Wealth Points are explained in more detail in later.

Determining Goals

Every character wants to achieve something. These desires drive you forward, giving meaning to your actions. Your goals are split into short-term and long-term ambitions.

Short-Term Ambition

A short-term ambition is something the character wants to happen soon. Ideally, a short-term ambition leads a character closer to completing a long-term ambition.

Long-Term Ambition

A long-term ambition is the character’s overarching goal. Achieving this goal should be considered a major turning point in that character’s life. Sometimes, it may be enough to convince a character to retire.

Achieving Goals

You gain experience when you complete a short-term ambition and create a new one. You and your entire group gain experience when you complete a long-term ambition. You can create a new ambition or allow the character to retire.

Experience and Growth

Several situations describe providing a character with experience. These are moments of development and advancement for characters. Experience allows a character to grow and learn from these moments. Each session provides at least one experience for the table just for participating. Characters also gain experience whenever the Game Master (GM) spends three or more Chaos at once. Every character in the group receives this experience. A character may have no more than nine experiences at one time. Any gain over this limit disappears and is no longer applicable.

Using Experience

Characters use their experiences to improve themselves. Different improvements will have different costs of experience. You may spend any amount of experience during a downtime period. It is vital to keep track of experience gained and paid as it may be helpful later in the character’s life.

Character Legacies

Sometimes, characters perish. Sometimes, they decide they’ve found a purpose other than adventuring. It's natural for characters to leave a group, which may lead to new opportunities for the players.

Retired Legends

A character may retire under two circumstances.

  • The character has achieved their long-term ambition.
  • The character has reached the end of an adventure and has three or more Injuries.

A retired character settles down in a nearby community of their choice and is no longer playable. They are instead an allied NPC. This causes a few changes for the group.

A Friendly Face

When the group visits their community, the retired friend guarantees shelter and hospitality. A long or short rest in a community with a retired friend restores an extra 50% of Hope (rounded up).

News and Information

The group may contact a retired friend once per session for help. Contact may be in person or by alternative means available to the group. The group may ask the retired friend three questions about the local area and recent events. The Game Master (GM) provides information in response to the group.

Your Turn to Carry This...

A retiring character selects one item and one bond they have. The bonded character receives the selected item and ignores any Technique Level requirements. Retiring characters may repeat this for each bond they have.

Shifted Bonds

Any character with a bond with the retired friend changes that bond to a Reminiscent Bond, as shown below.

Reminiscent Bond

Note down the highest attribute the former character has. Once per session, a character may recall their old friend. The character may roll a number of d12s based on the former character’s highest attribute. Each success generates a single Hope.

Fallen Legends

A fallen character lives on in the memories of their friends. Any character who had a bond to a fallen character changes that bond to the Memorial Bond below.

Memorial Bond

Note down the highest attribute and approach the former character has. Once per session, a character may recall their fallen friend. The character may roll a number of d12s based on the fallen character's highest attribute (approach). Every success grants a single point of Hope and reduces a single point of Chaos.

New Characters

The player may create a new character if their current character has retired or fallen. The player creates the character as they see fit. The new character is another legend adding to the world's legacy. A character created after the fall or retirement of another character gains experience. Any unspent experience of the former character goes to the new character. Half of the experience (rounded up) spent by the former character also goes to the new character.

Advanced Archetypes

An adventure may need more capable starting characters. Sometimes, players create a new character in an ongoing game. Some characters have enough experience and may have access to advanced archetypes. These are more capable or powerful character concepts. Advanced archetypes have distinct abilities and statuses for an adventure. A player may select an advanced archetype with the Game Master's (GM's) permission. The GM may have introduced the archetypes during the game or as an option after a character legacy. Characters often learn about these advanced archetypes through the story. The characters may have met a member or discovered information about these groups. The following Advanced Archetypes cannot be selected until Legendary talents have been introduced to a campaign. The Game Master may allow this archetype. A player must spend six experiences during character creation to choose one of these archetypes.
